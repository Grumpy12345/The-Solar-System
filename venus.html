Venus

Venus, sometimes called the evil twin of Earth, is the second and hottest planet in the solar system, and also has the thickest atmosphere. Named after the Roman goddess of love due to its bright appearance in the night sky, Venus is an interesting planet.

Formation

Like all the other planets in the Solar System, Venus was formed 4.5 billion years ago with the sun when gravity pulled a solar nebula together and it began to spin into a disk. Venus grew out of that disk with the sun and particles came together to form a planet with the smaller particles being blown away by solar winds. Like all the other terrestrial planets, Venus has a core, a mantle, and a crust. The core formed first then particles around it came to stick to it and formed the outer layers. It also collected nebulous particles and gases to form its thick atmosphere. There was and there still is a lot of carbon dioxide on Venus, which caused it to heat up to temperatures high enough to melt lead. Studies have shown that Venus may have had water on its surface 3 billion years ago. However, the water on Venus evaporated around 700 to 750 million years ago. The main reason why it happened was because lots of volcanoes pumped a great amount of carbon dioxide into the air, causing the temperatures to rise and the water to evaporate. It has also lost its water because the solar wind strikes Venus in the upper atmosphere and causes particles to be carried off into space. Unless the carbon dioxide decreases, Venus will not have liquid water on it again.

Appearance and Structure

Venus is the most spherical planet in the solar system with bulging equators and flatter poles, but its rotation allows it to be so round. The radius of Venus is 6051.8 kilometres, and the circumference is 38025.58 kilometres, almost the same size as Earth, and is the third smallest planet in the solar system. Venus weighs 4,867,000,000,000,000,000,000,000kg. From space, Venus looks bright white and orange because of its thick, nebulous atmosphere. The clouds in it reflect and gather sunlight and because of this, Venus would look bright orange if you were standing on it. On the surface, there are many rocks, but they are mostly different shades of grey. Venus has three layers, not including the atmosphere. There are two layers in the core: inner and outer. The inner core is made of iron and nickel and is solid because of the pressure on top of it, although it is not hot enough to melt. The outer core is also made of iron and nickel but is liquid because it is not put under enough pressure. The core has a radius of 3000km. It is 80 percent iron, 6 percent nickel, and some sulphur. Venus's mantle is thick and melted. It is made of a mixture of silicates and metallic oxides. There is no recognisable plate system. Venus's mantle is weaker than Earth's and approximately 2840 kilometres thick. Venus's crust is made of rock silicates and metals. On the surface, there are lots of volcanoes, large craters, several lowlands, two highlands, and mostly rolling plains. Venus has no moons or rings but might have had a moon in the past which got destroyed by crashing into the planet.

Orbit and Rotation

Venus is the second planet from the sun and 107.61 billion kilometres away from it, or 6 light minutes. It is also 240.11 million kilometres from the Earth and 107.987 million kilometres from Mercury. Venus has a strange orbit and rotation pattern because Venus travel around the sun clockwise, while most of the other planets travel anticlockwise (rotating the other way is called a retrograde rotation), so on Venus, the sun rises in the West and sets in the East. Venus also has long days and short years: in fact, one day on Venus is longer than one year! Venus has daytime for more than one year and nighttime for more than one year too. It takes about 243 Earth days for Venus to spin around its axis once, but 225 Earth days to travel once around the sun. It is the second fastest moving planet in the solar system and moves at an average speed of 126071.181 kilometres per hour. At some time in the past, another celestial body hit Venus, causing it to have a retrograde rotation. Because of this, its axes are flipped so the north pole is in the south and the south pole is in the north on Venus. This gives an axial tilt of 177 degrees.

Atmosphere and Weather

Venus has a thick, toxic atmosphere and the biggest atmosphere of the terrestrial planets. The atmospheric pressure is 92 times as much as Earth's, so if you stood on Venus, you would be squashed flat. The atmosphere is made of mostly carbon dioxide, so you cannot breathe on Venus. It is made of 96 percent carbon dioxide and 3.5 percent molecular nitrogen. It is 48 to 69 kilometres thick. The thick, dense atmosphere is divided into layers depending on its altitude. The lowest and densest part, the troposphere, starts at the surface and is 65 kilometres thick. Above that, there is a tropopause which separates the troposphere from the mesosphere, like the transition region on the sun. The next layer is the mesosphere which is 55 kilometres thick. Then there is a thermosphere which is 100 to 230 kilometres above the mesosphere. Finally, the last layer of the atmosphere is the exosphere. Beyond that is outer space. Because of the atmosphere, the weather is hot and unpleasant and Venus is the htotest planet in the solar system. It is around 464 degrees celsius and the lowest recorded temperature is negative 175 degrees celsius.
