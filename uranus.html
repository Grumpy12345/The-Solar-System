Uranus

Uranus is the coldest planet in the solar system, although it is not the furthest away from the sun. It has faint rings that people might not know about, and is both a gas giant and an ice giant.

Formation

Uranus is a gas giant but also an ice giant, because it is smaller than Jupiter and Saturn, colder, their interiors are different, and they were formed differently. There are two theories of how gas giants and ice giants were formed: the core accretion model and the disk instability model. The core accretion model is how the rocky core formed first from the solar nebula 4.5 billion years ago. When it became big enough, it attracted light gases that were not blown away by the solar winds. Its gravitational pull gathered up hydrogen, helium, and methane. This process would take several million years for Uranus to evolve into the planet it is now. The other theory, the disk instability model, is when the gas giants form really fast because clumps of gas and dust are already bonded together early in time and attract lighter gases as they grow bigger quickly, have a certain mass large enough so it would not be pulled into the sun and burned. Uranus was first observed in 1781 and it is named after the Greek god of the sky, Ouranos because of its colour.

Structure and Appearance

Uranus is a mostly blue-green colour, because the methane in Uranus absorbs red light from the sun but reflects blue light. Uranus is much bigger than Earth but smaller than Saturn and Jupiter and about 63 Earths can fit into Uranus and 22 Uranus can fit in the sun. Uranus has a radius of 25,362 kilometres, a circumference of 160,590 kilometres, and weighs 86,810,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kilograms. Uranus is made of mostly frozen water, methane, and ammonia and its atmosphere is made of molecular hydrogen, helium, and methane. There are three layers inside Uranus: a rocky core, a frozen mantle, and on the 'surface' is an envelope of hydrogen and helium. Uranus's core is freezing and rocky, made of silicate, iron, and nickel. The diameter of it is about 14,000 kilometres, which is a bit bigger than Earth. Above the core, there is an icy mantle that is 9,000 kilometres thick, but it's not actually ice. It is a hot, dense liquid made of flowing icy materials like ammonia. It is the thickest part of Uranus. The 'surface' is also the atmosphere and made of water, methane, and ammonia. The surface area of Uranus is 8.083 billion kilometres.

Orbit and Rotation

Uranus is the seventh planet from the sun. It has a strange orbit and rotation because it orbits in a different direction and rotates on its side. It takes 84 Earth years or 30,687 Earth years for Uranus to orbit once around the sun at a speed of 9,320 kilometres per hour. A day on Uranus lasts 17 hours, 14 minutes, and 24 seconds. The average temperature of Uranus is -213.889 degrees celsius and the coldest temperature recorded on Uranus is -223.889 degrees celsius. It is colder than Neptune not because of the distance from the sun, but because in the past, something crashed into Uranus (probably a protoplanet) and caused it to spin on its side. Its axial tilt is 98 degrees. Uranus has four seasons, with each of them 21 years long. Since Uranus has an extreme tilt, its seasons are also extreme. On Earth, there are polar nights and midnight suns only in the North and South poles. On Uranus, one season is one polar night or midnight sun, which lasts 21 years.

Atmosphere and Weather

Uranus has a thick atmosphere, which is also its surface. It is made of mostly hydrogen, with a bit of helium and methane. The temperature in the atmosphere is usually -224 degrees celsius. The atmosphere on Uranus is divided into three layers: the troposphere at the bottom, the stratosphere in the middle, and the outer atmosphere at the top (divided into the thermosphere and the exosphere), but unlike Earth's atmosphere, Uranus's atmosphere does not have a mesosphere, the region between the stratosphere and the thermosphere. The troposphere is the lowest and densest part of the atmosphere. It is between -500 kilometres to 50 kilometres thick. There are layers of clouds in the troposphere: normal clouds at low pressures, ammonium hydrosulphide above them,and a thin layer of methane above that. Between the troposphere and the stratosphere is a tropopause, like a transition region. The stratosphere is 50 to 4000 kilometres thick and usually -218 degrees celsius to -153 degrees celsius. It absorbs ultraviolet radiation and is made of hydrocarbons: acetylene and ethane, and also heavier elements like methylacetylene and diacetylene. There are also traces of carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide. The exosphere on Uranus is unusually extended a great distance into outer space and located at a height of 6,500 kilometres above the planet. Uranus also has many storms, like giant methane storms, because sunlight causes changes in its atmosphere. The storm on Uranus is called The Berg because it looked like an iceberg falling off an ice shelf. In the North pole, there is a huge, big white spot of storm clouds.

Bodies Orbiting It

Uranus has 13 thin, faint rings that are not as obvious as Saturn's. There are two sets of rings. The inner 9 rings are dark grey and narrow. The outer rings are brightly coloured. The system of rings was probably formed from colisions between moons that once orbited the planet. The outermost ring is made of long ice boulders, and the other rings are made of icy chunks that are darkened by rocks. Uranus has 27 moons which are named after characters from works of Shakespear and Alexander Pope. From largest to smallest, they are called Titania, Oberon, Umbriel, Miranda, Ariel, Puck, Desdemona, Cressida, Sycorax, Perdita, Setebos, Stephano, Caliban, Trinculo, Bianca, Cordelia, Ophelia, Mab, Juliet, Cupid, Rosalind, Francisco, Portia, Belinda, Margaret, and Ferdinand. They range from having a radius of 788.4 kilometres to a radius of 6 kilometres. Uranus's moons are elliptical and mostly spin in a retrograde direction, orbiting at large distances from the planet. The moons might have formed from collisions that knocked Uranus onto its side.
