The Sun

The Sun is a Yellow Dwarf star, and is the centre of our solar system. All the planets in our solar system revolves around the sun. The radius of the sun is estimated to be around 696,340 Km, but even though it may seem very big, our sun is actually tiny compared to some of the other stars in other solar systems. The Sun is over 6000 Degrees Celsius, but it isn't even close to being the hottest star in the galaxy. Without this star, we wouldn't exist.

Formation of the Sun

The sun was formed when the solar system was formed, 4.6 billion years ago, during the Part of Hall of the Universe. Waves of energy travelling in space pulled objects closer together until they formed a cloud of swirling gas and dust, called a solar nebula. Its gravity collapsed which caused it to spin faster into a disk. In the centre of it, material joined together to form a protostar, a contracting mass of gas, which was one of the early stages of the formation of a star. The protostar was a ball of mostly hydrogen and helium, but it was not yet powered by fusion. As the gases pulled together, spread, and increased, the temperature starts to increase too. Then a star would form when the temperature is hot enough for nuclear reactions to start, which is what powers the star as it releases energy and keeps the core hot. The outward pressure and the sun's huge gravitational pull keeps it stable and balanced. It is powered by hydrogen for a long time until the hydrogen runs out.

The Sun's Magnetic Field

The sun is the centre of the solar system so everything would float around in space without its huge magnetic field to keep it in place. Its magnetic field and radiation is what causes weather patterns on Earth. The sun is made of plasma, the fourth state of matter which is formed when electrons and ions in an atom have separated and caused the particles to be charged and extremely hot. The plasma flows inside the sun and is churned up by enormous heat created by nuclear fusion, or how the sun is powered, from the core of the sun. This complicated system, also known as the solar dynamo, is what creates the magnetic field of the sun. It stretches out around the whole solar system, stretching a total of 1 Gauss, which is 75-100 times the distance from the Earth to the sun. The magnetic field causes activity on the surface of the sun, creating a cycle called the Solar Cycle. The field has two poles, like a magnet. The poles flip every eleven years, at the peak of the solar activity cycle. It affects the activity on the surface of the sun and creates sunspots. The cycle starts from a Solar Minimum, which is when there are the least sunspots on the sun. Over time, the solar activity increases and so does the amount of sunspots. The middle of the cycle is called a Solar Maximum, the time when there are the most amount of sunspots on its surface. Then it starts to fade and the activity decreases, going back to a Solar Minimum and the cycle starts all over again. It takes about 11 years for one cycle to occur. A solar wind made of charged particles carry the gravitational pull away from the sun and into the solar system. The surface of the sun moves at different speeds depending on latitude. This results in magnetic fields getting wound up. When the winding gets extreme, the magnetic field lines snap, causing solar flares on those places on the surface. The suns's magnetic field also extends a bit beyond the planets into interstellar space. That region is called the heliosphere and acts like a shield against charged particles from space called cosmic rays which are dangerous.

Structure and Appearance

The sun shines brightly in the daytime and is the biggest and brightest thing in the sky during the daytime. Sunlight reaches Earth's atmosphere in the daytime and scatters in all directions by the gases and particles in the atmosphere. Blue light is scattered more than any other colour, which is what makes the sky blue. There are four layers in the sun: the core, the photosphere, the chromosphere, and the corona. The core is the centre of the sun. It has two layers: the radiative zone and the convective zone. The radiative zone is extremely hot and can reach up to 15 million degrees celsius. The convective zone is cooler. The sun's core is made of billions of billions of hydrogen atoms. The intense pressure and heat there pushes these atoms closer together so they get attracted and merge together to form new atoms. This is called nuclear fusion. It powers the sun. The photosphere is the next layer in the sun. It is the surface of the sun and we can see it with the naked eye. The photosphere is around 100 kilometres thick and is made of convection cells called granules, which are cells of plasma with hot rising plasma in the centre. Inside the photosphere, there is a lot of gas pressure and the energy is transferred to the magnetic field in a form called magnetohydrodynamic waves or electric currents. The next layer up is the chromosphere. It is the lower part of the sun's atmosphere and its name comes from the Greek root 'chroma', meaning 'colour', because it is bright red during a solar eclipse. It is about 2000 kilometres above the photosphere. The chromosphere has a very low density which is why we cannot see it in front of the bright photosphere. The temperature in the chromosphere is approximately 4000 to 6000 degrees Celsius with the higher temperatures around the photosphere. There is an extremely thin layer above called the transition region, which separates the chromosphere from the corona (the outermost layer of the sun and the upper atmosphere layer). The corona extends thousands of kilometres above the surface of the sun and slowly transforms into the solar winds that flow through the solar system. It is made of extremely hot but very weak plasma and is more than a million degrees there. However, the pressure and density there is much smaller than the one on Earth.

Location and Movement

The sun is centre of our solar system. It is in a galaxy called the Milky Way in a spiral arm called the Orion Spur. The sun orbits around the centre of the Milky Way, bringing with it the solar system. It moves with an average speed of 720,000 kilometres per hour but takes around 230 million years to make one complete trip around the Milky Way. The sun rotates on its axis, which is tilted approximately 7.25 degrees. Since the sun is not solid, different parts of it rotate at different speeds. It spins faster at its equator than at its poles. It rotates every 24 days at its equator, but every 35 days at its poles. The sun is near the Orion Arm, between the Perseus and Sagittarius. The sun is located in the Milky Way's galactic habitable zone. It is surrounded by a Local Interstellar Cloud, called the Local Bubble. The sun is about 26,660 light years from the Galactic centre.

Death of the Sun in the Future

Although the sun is big and has been around for millions of years, it will not last forever. It runs on hydrogen, but it will eventually run out, and the sun will die. Usually, when stars die, they explode into a supernova then turn into a black hole. But this is not the case for the sun because it does not have enough mass to do so. It will run out of hydrogen in the core in about 5 billion years and the nuclear fusion will stop so the core will shrink. This will release gravitational energy which will cause the sun to grow bigger into a subgiant over the next billion years and after that, a red giant. When it becomes a red giant, it will get so big it will pull Mercury and most likely Venus into it and spend around a billion years being a red giant then lose a third of its mass. After that, the core ignites in a helium flash, and 6 percent of it will be converted into carbon. Then it will shrink 10 times its current size and will continue to get rid of helium in its core until its helium runs out too. It will grow larger and brighter and after a many years of a lot more movement, and start to make a planetary nebula which will disperse in about 10,000 years.
