Neptune

Although Neptune is the furthest away from the sun, it is not the coldest planet. Neptune was named after the Roman god of the sea, or the Greek god Poseidon, because of its deep blue colour. It is a darker blue than Uranus but the reason behind that is still unknown.

Formation

Neptune formed in a similar way to Uranus: either from the core accretion model, or the gas instability model. The core accretion model usually takes a few million years to complete, but the debris orbiting the sun didn't last that long so the gas giants and ice giants needed to form quickly. If Neptune formed from the core accretion model, the core formed first like the cores of the terrestrial planets then attracted other gases towards it before the solar winds blew them away. In order for this to work for Neptune, it would need to move as well. If this was how Neptune was formed, it would have travelled away from the sun to its present position once the gaseous disk dissipated. Parts of gas and dust clumped together and grow quickly, allowing them to trap light gases that were rapidly disappearing. After the gas giants formed quickly, something pulled them outwards (most likely small gravitational tugs from passing comets, which would have taken a long time) to their current positions.

Structure and Appearance

Neptune is mostly dark blue because of its methane atmosphere absorbing red and infrared light. The radius of Neptune is at its poles is slightly smaller than at its equator, but the average is 24,341 kilometres. The average diameter is 49,224 kilometres, the surface area is 7.618 billion kilometres squared, and the weight is 102,400,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kilograms. Neptune's atmosphere is made of 80 percent hydrogen, 19 percent helium, methane, and hydrogen deuteride. Neptune's interior is made of icy materials: frozen water, methane, and ammonia, making it also an ice giant. Neptune has no surface because it is a gas giant. It has a solid core, a mantle, an atmosphere, and an upper atmosphere. The atmosphere is made of gases. Neptune's core is made of iron and other metals, and is almost as heavy as our planet. The temperatures there can reach 5,127 degrees celsius. The radius of Neptune's core is 24,764 kilometres. Neptune's mantle is a dense (it is so dense it contains 80 percent of Neptune's mass) liquid made of water, ammonia, and methane. The temperature there is 4,724 degrees celsius and it is ten times heavier than Earth. There is no crust or surface, but an atmosphere above that.

Orbit and Rotation

Neptune is 4.474 billion kilometres away from the sun, or 252.1 light minutes, and Neptune is 1,627,450,000 kilometres away from Uranus. Neptune moves at a speed of 2.68 kilometres per second, or 9,650 kilometres per hour. It takes 165 Earth years to orbit once around the sun and a day on Neptune is 16 hours and 6 minutes. A day on Neptune is still dark because it is so far away from the sun, so only about a thousandth of the sunlight on Earth reaches Neptune. It is called a Neptunian day. Neptune spins in an anticlockwise direction like most of the other planets and the axial tilt of Neptune is 28 degrees, similar to Earth and Mars. Neptune experiences four seasons like Earth does, but since it is so far away, each season lasts for 40 years. Seasonal drift produces Summer and Winter in different atmospheres with Spring and Autumn in between. Even in Summer, Neptune is actually becoming colder, except for its poles which are warming up, probably because of changes in its atmospheric chemistry.

Atmosphere and Weather

Neptune has a thick atmosphere which is made of mostly hydrogen, with smaller amounts of helium and methane. There are two main atmosphere layers on Neptune: the troposphere and the stratosphere. There are many clouds in Neptune's troposphere. The upper clouds are made of mostly methane, which give it a blue colour. It is also the right temperature there for methane to condense. Below that, there are clouds of ammonia and hydrogen sulphide. There are also clouds made of ammonia, ammonium hydrosulphide, and hydrogen sulphide. In the troposphere, between altitudes of 50 to 150 kilometres, that is where the weather happens. There is no weather anywhere else in the atmosphere. As the layers in the atmosphere are higher, the temperature decreases. Neptune has the strongest winds in the solar system and very powerful storms. The speed of the winds can reach 2,100 kilometres per hour, three times as fast as Jupiter's winds, and nine times as fast as Earth's winds. Storms on Neptune have winds travelling backwards in high pressure systems. There was a Great Dark spot on Neptune where the most powerful storms occurred, in Neptune's southern hemisphere. It was about the size of Earth, but it slowly disappeared and before 1990.

Bodies Orbiting It

Neptune has 14 moons, which are Naiad, Thalassa, Despina, Galatea, Larissa, S/2004 N1 (which does not have a name yet), Proteus, Triton, Nereid, Halimede, Sao, Laomedeia, Psamathe, and Neso. The largest moon is Triton. It is the only round moon of Neptune because of its size, and all the others are irregular shapes because they do not have enough gravity. They are mostly made of frozen water contaminated with a very dark material. Neptune also has 9 faint rings. There are five main rings, which are named Galle, Leverrier, Lassell, Arago, and Adams. They are made of ice particles and silicates, are mostly a reddish colour, and formed when one of Neptune's moons got to close and was torn apart into many pieces by Neptune's gravity. Neptune's rings are believed to be very young and much younger than the solar system and one part of it is actually disappearing.
