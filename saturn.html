Saturn

Saturn, the sixth planet from the sun, is known for its large, beautiful rings. It a gas giant named after the Roman god of agriculture and wealth. There are still many things unknown about this planet.

Formation

Saturn was formed 4.5 billion years ago and grew out of the solar nebula that formed the sun and the solar system. Saturn is a gas giant like Jupiter, and was formed in a similar way with the solid core formed first then gases gathering around to make it bigger and being attracted by its gravitational pull. Further away from the sun, the solar winds had less impact on light materials such as hydrogen and helium, so Saturn's gravitational pull had a bigger impact on it and pulled it to surround the core, forming a gas giant. This kept on happening for several million years for Saturn to evolve into the planet it is today. Saturn's rings formed 10 million to 100 million years ago and are made up of pieces of comets, asteroids, meteors, shattered moons, or other pieces of things in space that were crashing into Saturn but the was broken up by the atmospheric pressure and stayed in a location around Saturn where its gravitational pull caused it to stay there. Over time, they became coated with other materials such as dust.

Structure and Appearance

Saturn is the second biggest planet in the solar system. It has a radius of 58,232 kilometres, a diameter of 116,460 kilometres, a circumference of 378,675 kilometres, a surface area of 42.7 billion kilometres, and a weight of 568,300,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kilograms. Saturn is made of approximately 75 percent hydrogen and 25 percent helium, but there are traces of other gases in it like methane and there are crystals of ammonia in its upper atmosphere which give it its pale yellow colour. When seen from a distance, it is pale yellow and brown, but when seen up close, it also has other small colours like red, brown, and white spots which change over short periods of time. Saturn also has lots of storm clouds, which are hard to see because of its colour. It has a core, a layer of metallic hydrogen, a layer of liquid hydrogen and liquid helium, and an outer layer made of gases. The core is solid and dense, made of mostly iron and nickel with other rocky elements. It had become solid because of the intense pressure and heat in there, and temperatures can rise to 35,700 degrees Celsius, hotter than the surface of the sun! From the core to the atmosphere of Saturn, the elements change as the temperature decreases from solid, to liquid, to gas. Surrounding the core is liquid layer, which is made of metallic hydrogen near the core, a transition region, and molecular hydrogen near the parts that lead to where the state of matter is gas. Above that is the gases, which eventually lead to the atmosphere, and can also be called the surface of Saturn. There is no solid surface so if you stood on Saturn, you would sink down into the lower layers and die from the intense pressure.

Saturn's rings are quite well known. They are about 282,000 km wide but only 10m thick. The rings are made out of ice and rock, and so you cannot stand on it. The floating matter in the rings are held together with gravitational pull from Saturn Itself. Some smaller moons have settled into Saturn's Rings.

Orbit and Rotation

Saturn is the sixth planet from the sun and approximately 1.4742 billion kilometres away from the sun and the closest planet to Saturn is Jupiter. Saturn takes 10.7 hours to rotate once around its own axis and takes 29.4 Earth years to orbit once around the sun, spinning at an average speed of 34,821 kilometres per hour. Saturn has the second shortest days and nights of the planets, with one day being about 10 Earth hours. The temperature during the day is -176.111 degrees celsius and the temperature during the night is -191 degrees celsius. The highest temperature recorded on Saturn is 11,700 degrees celsius at its core, and the average temperature on Saturn is about -176 degrees celsius. The axial tilt of Saturn is 26.73 degrees and is caused by the migration of its satellites, or moons, especially of Titan, its largest moon. Saturn has four seasons and each season is around 7 years long.

Atmosphere and Weather

Saturn has a thick, nebulous atmosphere, which is about 200 kilometres thick with a surface pressure of 140kPa. It is 75 percent hydrogen and 25 percent helium. The atmosphere is where most of the gas is on Saturn. There is an exosphere made of mostly hydrogen and a bit of helium, and a troposphere made of similar layers of clouds called cloud decks. There are three layers of cloud decks which are made of different molecules. The first cloud deck is made of mostly ammonia, the second cloud deck is made of ammonium hydrosulphide clouds, a combination of ammonia and sulphur, and the third cloud deck is made of ordinary clouds. It rains carbon on Saturn and it is very stormy there. Storms on Saturn are called 30-year storms. They are like hurricanes and typhoons but last longer. There are different storm patterns on Saturn. In the North pole, the storms are in a hexagonal pattern, and in the South pole, the storms are like streams. Occasionally, there are white spots on Saturn's surface and a powerful storm starts.

Bodies Orbiting It

Saturn has the most moons in the solar system, with 82 moons known. Saturn's moons all have different sizes. Some of them are tiny bits of rock, and some of them are even larger than Mercury! The larger moons are in a spherical shape, and the smaller moons are in weird shapes that look more like asteroids than moons. The five biggest moons are Titan, Dione, Enceladus, Hyperion, and Iapetus. Saturn's moons were formed from leftover pieces from larger bodies. Saturn got so many moons because of its strong gravitational pull, attracting bodies from far away. Only 7 moons are visible in the night sky with the naked eye, but it is possible to see them from a telescope. The easiest moon to see is Titan because it is the biggest moon. Titan actually has liquid seas on it, but they are filled with ethane, methane, and propane. Some moons actually have a very thin oxygen atmosphere, the only other known place with oxygen in the solar system.
