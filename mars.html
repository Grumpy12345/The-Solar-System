Mars

Mars is named after the Roman god of war because of its red colour. It is the fourth planet from the sun and second smallest planet, which people hope to move to live on. There are still many things unknown about this planet.

Formation

Mars formed and grew out of the solar nebula when the solar system was formed, but unlike Mercury and Venus, where particles clumped together from gravity over time, Mars was formed from collisions between giant rocks the size of a moon. Mars and Earth both formed from similar materials, and out of all the other planets in the solar system, Mars is the most like Earth. 4.5 billion years ago, in the ancient Noachian time period, the environment on Mars had liquid water, a thick atmosphere, and could have been habitable for microorganisms, unlike the planet we see today. However, a huge event has happened to cause Mars to be cold and dry with a thin atmosphere. Most of the atmosphere on Mars has been lost in space by being blown away by solar winds, because the gravitational pull was not strong enough. This was because when Mars's molten-iron core cooled and solidified, the magnetic field dissipated. 65 percent of the argon in the atmosphere was lost in space. Without a stable atmosphere, the water disappeared. It also disappeared because there was an increasing amount of carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas, about 3 billion years ago. Dust storms have also caused water to dry up.

Appearance and Structure

Mars is the second smallest planet in the solar system. The radius of Mars is 3,389.5 kilometres, the circumference is 21,344 kilometres, and Mars weighs 639,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kilograms. Mars is known as the red planet because of its rusty red colour, which is iron oxide. It has a layer of rusty dust covering its surface. Other colours on its surface include golden, brown, and tan. Although it appears red from a far distance, when you look at it closely, it is more of a orangish-brown. The internal structure of Mars is similar to the other terrestrial planets. There is a metal core in the centre, a mantle, and a thin rocky crust. The radius of the core is 1810 to 1860 kilometres and is made of metallic iron and nickel which used to be molten before the cooling. The temperature in the core is 1076.85 degrees celsius, the hottest part of Mars. The mantle is made of silicon, oxygen, iron, and magnesium and has the consistency of a soft, rocky paste. It is about 1240 to 1880 kilometres thick. The crust is 24 to 72 kilometres thick, with the crust in the south being thicker and older than the crust in the north. There are no tectonic plates on Mars. There are layers of dust in the crust but beneath that, there are volcanic rocks. The soil of Mars contains sodium, potassium, chloride, and magnesium. Landforms on Mars include dark slope streaks, dust devil tracks, sand dunes, fretted terrain, layers, gullies, glaciers, scalloped topography, chaos terrain, possibly ancient rivers, pedestal craters, brain terrain, and ring mould craters. Mars also has the tallest mountain in the solar system: Olympus Mons.

Orbit and Rotation

Mars is the fourth planet from the sun and 211.13 million kilometres, or 12 light minutes and 42 light seconds. Mars orbits at a speed of 86,871 kilometres per hour, and takes 687 days to make one full rotation around the sun and spins in an anticlockwise direction. Mars takes 24.6 hours to spin once around its own axis, so one day on Mars is almost the same length as one day on Earth. Martian days are called 'solar days'. Mars's axial tilt is also very similar to Earth's - Mars is tilted 25 degrees while Earth is tilted 23 degrees. Mars also has the complete four seasons, but are different to Earth's because of the orbital speed. Spring is 7 months long, Summer is 6 months long, Autumn is 5 months, and Winter is 4 months. In summer, the temperatures rise to about 20 degrees celsius during the day but at night, the temperatures still drop to -73 degrees celsius. In Winter near the poles, the temperatures can drop to -125 degrees celsius. The coldest temperature recorded on Mars is -153 degrees celsius. Mars is cold because its atmosphere is thin and Mars can't trap heat as much as Earth. On Mars, it actually snows sometimes, but the snow evaporates before it reaches the ground. It does not rain on Mars, but there are huge winds that cause giant dust storms and the winds are still changing the landforms on Mars.

Bodies Orbiting Around It

Mars has no rings but it has two small moons: Deimos and Phobos. They were named after the sons of the Roman god of war, because Mars is named after the god of war. The moons of Mars are the smallest moons in the solar system. Phobos is a bit larger than Deimos, but both look more like asteroids than moons because of their long shapes. They are not round because there is not enough gravity to overcome the rock stresses, but they are lumpy with many big craters from collisions with asteroids and covered with dust and loose rocks. They are both made of carbon-rich rock mixed with ice from asteroids and have formed from a huge collision from a protoplanet. The radius of Phobos is 11.267 kilometres and the radius of Deimos is 6.2 kilometres. Deimos is red and most of Phobos is red like Mars. The moons don't look like moons in the night sky. Even from Mars, they look more like stars and when Deimos is shining at its brightest in the sky, it look like Venus. The moons also go through phases through the short time they take to orbit around Mars. But we cannot see them go through the whole cycle because Mars orbits the sun behind Earth.

Atmosphere and Weather

Mars used to have a thick atmosphere that kept with greenhouse gases that kept its water from freezing or evaporating, but it has been blown away by solar winds since Mars does not have enough gravity to keep it. Mars is less that half Earth's size and about a tenth of its mass so there is less gravitational pull. Mars's atmosphere is only a hundredth of Earth's and can only retain an atmosphere of 0.38 bar. The atmosphere on Mars now is made of 95 percent carbon dioxide, 2.6 percent molecular nitrogen, 1.9 percent argon, 0.16 percent molecular oxygen, and 0.06 percent carbon monoxide. Mars's atmosphere is made of three layers: the troposphere, the mesosphere, and the exosphere. The troposphere is 40 kilometres thick and absorbs solar radiation then re-emits it at long wavelengths. The mesosphere is around 60 kilometres thick, and the exosphere starts at a height of 230 kilometres and gradually merges into outer space. The weather is usually cold because of the thin atmosphere that cannot retain heat well, and there is a lot of wind on Mars which pick up particles of rock and dust and create huge dust storms. Mars is famous for its dust storms. There are three main types of dust storms: local dust storms, regional dust storms, and planet-encircling dust storms. Local dust storms are the smallest, usually under 2000 kilometres, and are basically hazes and clouds with a spatial dimension. Regional dust storms are over 2000 kilometres, and planet-encircling dust storms cover the whole planet in dust and wind.
