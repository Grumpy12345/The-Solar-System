Jupiter>

Did you know Jupiter is the fastest spinning (spinning, not moving) planet in the solar system? Jupiter is the fifth planet in the solar system and the largest planet. It is also an outer planet and a gas giant. There are many things about this massive planet that are still unknown.

Formation

Jupiter was formed 4.5 billion years ago from a solar nebula when the solar system was formed. But unlike the terrestrial planets, Jupiter was formed differently because it is a gas giant. It started to form from the debris orbiting the young sun which was full of gases. A core was made first, which was created from collisions amongst asteroids and comets and grows bigger from the debris in the solar nebula. Then when the core was big enough and had enough mass, its gravitational pull attracted gas to form the planet. At first, the cores were rocky and icy just like the terrestrial planets, but a lot bigger. These cores had enough gravitational pull to attract gases such as hydrogen and helium that was surrounding it. Since they had bigger cores, they were able to pull together enough gas before the sun formed and the solar winds blew away the remaining debris. When Jupiter started growing larger, the oceans boiled and the atmosphere became a dense mixture of steam, hydrogen, and helium. It grew rapidly, faster than the solid core, and eventually formed a gas giant.

Appearance and Structure

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system. The elements it is composed of creates clouds and then causes it to have stripes of white, orange, brown, and red. When viewed from Earth, it is a light tan colour. Jupiter has a diameter of 139,820 kilometres, a radius of 69,9911 kilometres, a circumference of 439,264 kilometres, and a mass of 1,898,130,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kilograms. Over 1,300 Earths can fit inside Jupiter, and around 1,000 Jupiters can fit into the sun. Jupiter is made mostly of hydrogen and helium. It is made of 88-92 percent hydrogen, 8-12 percent helium of gas molecules, and about 75 percent hydrogen, 24 percent helium, and the remaining one percent of other elements. The interior has denser materials, with 71 percent hydrogen, 24 percent helium, and 5 percent other elements. There is no solid surface layer on gas giants, but there is an increase of gases from the top of its atmosphere. The gases inside Jupiter gradually change to liquids, so gas giants don't actually have layers inside them like terrestrial planets do. The liquids of Jupiter are the biggest parts of it but they are not made of water - they are made of hydrogen and helium, because the pressure and temperature increase, compressing the gases into liquids. The first layer inside Jupiter is right under its atmosphere, and is called the liquid hydrogen layer. Under that is a liquid metallic hydrogen layer, and in the centre is the core, which is actually solid and about the size of Earth, but 14 to 18 times the mass. Jupiter also has tiny, almost invisible rings.

Orbit and Rotation

Jupiter is 741.91 million kilometres away from the sun and the fifth planet in the solar system. It takes 12 years for Jupiter to orbit the sun once, at a speed of 205996.03200000000652 kilometres per hour, but it takes only 10 Earth hours to rotate once around its own axis. Jupiter has an axial tilt of 3.13 degrees, so there are barely any difference in the seasons on Jupiter, but it does have a stormy weather all the time. The temperature around the equator is much hotter than around the poles, and the temperature in the core is actually hotter than the surface of the sun: 1797760 degrees celsius. The highest temperature recorded on Jupiter is 725 degrees celsius, in its atmosphere, and the coldest temperature recorded is -145 degrees celsius. The average temperature on Jupiter is -110 degrees celsius.

Bodies Orbiting It

Jupiter has the second most moons, with 79 moons in total and 53 named moons. The moons were named after Jupiter's daughters or people he liked. Some moons include Europa, Ganymede, Io, Callisto, Amalthea, Adrastea, Thebe, Valetudo, Elara, and Carme. The four main moons and largest moons are Europa, Ganymede, Io, and Callisto. They all have a diameter of over 3100 kilometres, and Ganymede is even larger than Mercury. If they orbited the sun instead of Jupiter, some of them could be planets. They also have a similar interior structure to Earth, with a core, a mantle with a bit of molten rock, and a solid crust. The other moons are all less than 250 kilometres in diameter, and orbital periods range from seven hours to almost three Earth years. Each moon turns once on its axis for every orbit around Jupiter. From Earth through a telescope, they look like tiny bits of light. They are all different colours. The four biggest moons of Jupiter are round like balls, but the rest of them are too small and light to have enough gravitational pull, resulting in irregular moon shapes. Jupiter's moons were formed from a circumplanetary disk, a ring shaped accumulation of matter orbiting around a planet. When Jupiter was formed with gas from the solar nebula, the leftover particles connected together over time, grew bigger, and became Jupiter's moons because of the gravitational pull.

Atmosphere and Weather

Since Jupiter is a gas giant and has no solid surface, its atmosphere is hard to distinguish from the planet because it gradually fades into the gases of the planet. Jupiter's atmosphere is extremely dense and also dry, made of 90 percent hydrogen, 10 percent helium, and a tiny bit of other gases such as methane and ammonia. The atmosphere is divided into four layers: the troposphere, the stratosphere, the thermosphere, and the exosphere. The region immediately above the 'surface' of Jupiter is the troposphere and is 50 kilometres thick. It contains ammonia, and ammonium hydrosulphide. The stratosphere is immediately above the troposphere and is also around 500 kilometres thick. It is warm in the stratosphere because of the methane there, but is also warmed by the sun, by Jupiter's core, and cooled by the gases surrounding it. The thermosphere is almost at the top of the atmosphere, with a thin exosphere, but the exosphere does not have definite boundaries and fades off into space. The weather on Jupiter is mostly stormy because there is no land and the weather is caused by the internal heat of Jupiter. It does not snow on Jupiter because there is not enough water deficiency and it cannot rain because there is not enough moisture. The biggest storm on Jupiter, and the biggest storm in the solar system, is the Great Red Spot. It is like a hurricane on Earth, but much larger and more powerful. In fact, the size of the Great Red Spot is twice as big as Earth! Winds inside the storm reach 434.523 kilometres per hour. In the past few years, however, the Great Red Spot has been shrinking and scientists predict that it will disappear in about 20 years.
