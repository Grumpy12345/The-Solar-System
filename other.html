Other Things in Space

Dwarf Planets

Dwarf planets are objects in space that have an orbit, but cannot be classified as a planet. These include Pluto (The most famous Dwarf Planet that most people know), Ceres, Makemake, Haumea, and Eris. These five are the officially recognised Dwarf Planets. Pluto, Makemake and Haumea are all 'Plutoids', which means that they orbit around the sun outside of Neptune. Ceres, on the other hand, orbits the sun in the Asteroid belt. Ceres is the only dwarf planet in the asteroid belt. They all break one of the three rules that classifies a celestial body as a planet.

The three rules of being a planet are that It must have a constant orbit around a star, it must have enough gravity to be able to pull itself into a spherical shape, and must be the biggest thing in its orbit and push everything else out of its way. The Third rule is where Pluto, and most other Dwarf planets, fail. They are often very small, and unable to push everything in its orbit out of it's way. A few Dwarf planets also have moons.

Dwarf Planets are a lot like regular planets. They have enough Gravity to pull themselves into a spherical shape, like a regular planet, which bumpy asteroids and comets can't do. Most Dwarf Planets look a lot like a regular moon of a planet and Mercury, as they are often small, greyish, and rocky.

Asteroids

Asteroids are small, rocky objects that orbit the sun. They are not big enough to be planets and do not orbit around a planet so they are not moons. There are over 822,000 asteroids in the solar system. The size of an asteroid can range from having a radius of 1 metre to having a radius of 470 kilometres. A few asteroids that are large enough are round, but most of them have irregular shapes. Their shape is determined by their mass and their gravitational pull. They also orbit in orbits that aren't very smooth, tumbling as they move. They formed 4.5 billion years ago from the pieces of the solar nebula that were left over. They also collided with each other to either break apart or become bigger. There are the most asteroids in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. Some asteroids include Pallas, Vesta, Hygiea, Aten asteroid, Eros, Amor asteroid, Geographos, and Eumonia.

Comets

A comet is a cosmic ball that orbit the sun with an elongated orbital route. They are like asteroids but they are made of a mixture of frozen gases, ice, and rock, instead of just rock. They were formed a similar way to asteroids. As they get closer to the sun, their ice and dust begin to melt which gives them a tail. Other particles also begin to melt, which creates a cloud around the nucleus, called a coma. Comets are usually irregular in shape and the average size is about 5 kilometres in radius. The probability of a comet crashing into Earth is very small and this probably will not happen for a few hundred years, but if it does, it could cause great damage. Halley's comet is one of the biggest comets. Some examples of comets are Halley's comet, Comet ISON, Comet Hale-Bopp, Tempel-1, Siding Spring, and Comet Encke.

Meteoroids

Meteoroids are small rocky or metallic bodies orbiting the sun. They are a lot smaller than asteroids and different from comets because they are made of rock and usually form by breaking off asteroids. When they crash into Earth and enter the atmosphere, they are called meteors and they burn up creating a streak of light. This is also known as shooting stars. When they have gone through the atmosphere without being destroyed and they hit the ground, they are called meteorites. They can kill many things and if they are big, they can possibly destroy a city. Meteoroids mostly orbit the sun among the terrestrial planets. Many of them are formed when asteroids collide with each other and produce debris, creating meteoroids. Their size can range from being as small as a grain to being one metre long.

Stars

A star is a body in space made of plasma held together by its gravitational pull. It has a huge magnetic field, pulling things around it to orbit it. Our sun is an example of a star and is the nearest star to Earth. Most stars are very far away from Earth, or we would be attracted to it. We can see them as specks of light in the night sky. All stars are formed from huge clouds of gas and dust, or nebulae. An average star forms then when it runs out of power it turns into a subgiant, a red giant, a planetary nebula, then a white dwarf, a white star about the size of a planet. If a giant star forms from a stellar nebula, it turns into a massive star and when it runs out of fuel it will expand to a red supergiant then explode into a supernova, ending up as either a blackhole or a neutron star. A black hole is so dense it sucks everything into it, even light. They will become a neutron star if they do not have enough mass to turn into a black hole. The hottest stars are blue, then white, then yellow, orange, and finally red. Blue stars have a temperature of between 9726.85 degrees Celsius and 49726.85 degrees Celsius.


Galaxies

A galaxy is a system of stars, space dust, solar systems, gas, and dark matter. There are many galaxies in the world and they can be huge. The galaxy we are in is the Milky Way. Galaxies are formed from huge clouds of dust and gas that rotate. As galaxies get bigger, stars and other celestial bodies form around inside them. There are four main types of galaxies: spiral, elliptical, peculiar, and irregular. The Milky Way is a spiral galaxy. Spiral galaxies have spiral arms and the stars and gas clouds are usually concentrated in one arm. Examples of spiral galaxies include: the Milky Way, Pinwheel galaxy, Whirlpool galaxy, Andromeda galaxy, and Messier 74. Elliptical galaxies have an ellipsoidal shape and is smooth. They include: M87 (NGC 4486), NCG5 (Adromeda), and NCG 67 (Adromeda). Peculiar galaxies are galaxies with abnormal size, shape, or composition. There are between five and ten percent of galaxies that are peculiar. Irregular galaxies have no distinct shape, but peculiar galaxies have shapes.
